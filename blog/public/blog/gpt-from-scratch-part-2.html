Building a GPT From Scratch, Part 2: Probability Distributions, Softmax, and Temperature

Before touching any generation code, I wanted gut-level intuition for how a model’s raw scores become the word it actually picks — and how a single “temperature” number reshapes that choice from decisive to adventurous.

In Part 1, I laid out the four-problem shape of a GPT-like chatbot: data, tokenization, generation, and the app wrapped around it. Step three on my build order was adding temperature, repetition penalty, and logit bias to a basic decode loop — but before writing any of that code, I stopped to make sure I actually understood the math it leans on. This post is that groundwork: probability distributions, softmax, and temperature, in that order.

Probability distribution: splitting 100% across the options

An LLM’s whole job, at generation time, is predicting what word comes next. A probability distribution is just the formal way of expressing “how likely is each option,” where every likelihood is non-negative and the whole set adds up to 1. Roll a fair die and you get the distribution {1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6}. Ask a language model what token comes next, and it hands back a distribution over its entire vocabulary — one probability per possible next token.

Ask a friend “what should we eat tonight?” and their preferences might work out to 50% pizza, 30% sushi, 20% tacos. That’s a probability distribution — a way of splitting “how likely” across options so it all adds up to 100%.

Softmax: turning messy scores into clean percentages

Neural networks don’t output probabilities directly. They output raw, unbounded numbers called logits — something like [2.0, 1.0, 0.1]. Softmax is the function that converts logits into a valid probability distribution:

softmax(xi) = exi / Σj exj

Three things happen in that formula:

  1. Each logit gets exponentiated, which makes every value positive.
  2. Everything is divided by the sum of all the exponentials, so the results add up to exactly 1.
  3. Because exponentials amplify differences, larger logits get disproportionately larger probabilities — the ranking is preserved, but the gaps widen.

So [2.0, 1.0, 0.1] becomes roughly [66%, 24%, 10%]. The highest logit dominates, but the rest still get some probability mass instead of being zeroed out.

Back to the dinner analogy: before your friend turns their gut feeling into neat percentages, their brain has messy scores that don’t add up to anything — say pizza = 8, sushi = 5, tacos = 3. Softmax is the recipe that converts those messy scores into clean percentages that sum to 100%, while keeping the same ranking.

Temperature: the dial between decisive and adventurous

Temperature is a scaling factor applied to the logits before softmax runs:

softmax(xi / T)

  • T = 1 — standard softmax, no change.
  • T < 1 (e.g. 0.5) — sharpens the distribution. The model gets more confident, favoring the top option more strongly. As T → 0, it approaches always picking the single most likely token (argmax) — deterministic, and often repetitive.
  • T > 1 (e.g. 2.0) — flattens the distribution. Probability mass spreads out toward less-likely options — more varied, more creative, but more prone to incoherence.

Temperature doesn’t change what the model prefers — the ranking of options never moves. It changes how strongly that preference gets acted on.

A worked example: dinner with logits

Same friend, same decision, now with actual gut-feeling scores (logits) for four options:

FoodLogit
pizza4.0
sushi3.0
tacos2.0
tofu bowl1.0

T = 1 (neutral): pizza 64%, sushi 24%, tacos 9%, tofu bowl 3%. Pizza dominates, but everyone else still gets a fair shot.

T = 0.5 (low, sharper): dividing every logit by 0.5 doubles them to [8, 6, 4, 2], which softmax turns into pizza 88%, sushi 12%, tacos 0.7%, tofu bowl 0.1%. Your friend becomes decisive to the point of predictable — pizza, pizza, pizza.

T = 2 (high, flatter): dividing by 2 gives [2.0, 1.5, 1.0, 0.5], which softmax turns into pizza 46%, sushi 28%, tacos 17%, tofu bowl 10%. The weakest option, tofu bowl, climbs from 3% all the way to 10%. Your friend gets more spontaneous, more willing to try something other than the favorite.

The pattern: as temperature rises, probability mass flows away from the favorite and toward the underdogs. The ranking never flips — pizza is always on top — only the strength of commitment to it changes. That’s exactly the knob I’ll be wiring into the decode loop next: low temperature for safe, deterministic text; high temperature for text that takes more risks with less-likely words.
