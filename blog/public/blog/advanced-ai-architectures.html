AI System Design: Part-4 - Advanced AI Architectures: RAG, Recommendations, and Real-Time Systems

The patterns behind three of the most common — and most misimplemented — AI systems in production.

The patterns behind three of the most common — and most misimplemented — AI systems in production.

There's the version of AI system design that lives on whiteboards: clean diagrams, one-directional arrows, components doing one thing. Then there's the version that runs in production — hybrid architectures, failure modes that surface only under real traffic, decisions that look wrong in isolation but right in context. This post covers three key patterns: big-data processing (Lambda/Kappa), real-time recommendations, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).

Part 1: Lambda vs. Kappa

At tens of millions of users you need two things at once: fresh insight into what's happening now, and accurate answers over all history. Batch processing is accurate but lags by hours; streaming is current but typically on a bounded recent window. Lambda Architecture (Nathan Marz, 2011) was the answer to having both.

Lambda runs two pipelines over the same data: a batch layer recomputing accurate views nightly, and a speed layer producing approximate current views. The serving layer merges them. Netflix used it for years — Hadoop nightly, Kafka+Flink for recent signals. The fatal flaw: the same business logic runs twice, in two systems, maintained by two teams. They drift. One 2024 post-mortem described batch and speed layers that diverged so badly over three years they gave different answers to the same query — and nobody noticed.

Kappa (Jay Kreps) simplifies: treat everything as a stream, including history, by replaying the event log at high parallelism. One codebase, one engine. Kafka's retention makes it viable — replay 90 days through Flink at 100× speed to rebuild views. Where it struggles: massive historical backfills (90-day retention isn't three years) and deep analytical queries that are awkward in streaming semantics.

The 2026 consensus — Kappa + Lakehouse: Kafka as the streaming backbone for real-time work, plus a Lakehouse (Delta, Iceberg) as the durable analytical store with ACID and time-travel. Pipelines periodically materialize streaming results into the Lakehouse. Not a clean principle — the pragmatic answer from teams needing both real-time and historically accurate systems that stay maintainable.

Part 2: Real-Time Recommendations

Conceptually simple — given a user, return ranked items. At scale (Netflix: 17,000 titles to 200M users) it requires careful staging. The naive approach of scoring every item for every user fails: at 100ms/item over 1M items, that's 28 hours per request. The production answer is a funnel that narrows candidates while increasing ranking sophistication.

Stage 1 — Retrieval. Narrows millions to hundreds via ANN search over embeddings. Users and items are encoded into dense vectors; similar ones sit close in the space. At serving, the user embedding queries a vector DB (HNSW/IVF) for the K nearest items in 5–20ms regardless of catalog size — ANN doesn't scan everything. FAISS builds the indexes; Pinecone/Qdrant host them. The embedding model matters enormously: items filtered out here can't be recovered by any downstream ranking.

Stage 2 — Ranking. Re-scores the hundreds with features too expensive to compute for millions: user features, item features, and cross features (does this user engage with this content type? seen it before?). Cross features make ranking powerful — and are exactly what retrieval can't afford. Runs on GPU; 100 candidates complete in under 20ms.

Stage 3 — Business logic. Filters scores through diversity constraints, freshness boosts, inventory checks, regulatory filters, and margin rules. Deliberately not a model — explicit, auditable code. When a PM says "too many items from one category," the fix is a diversity filter here, not a retrain.

Feature boundaries. Batch features (30-day genre preferences) are precomputed and cached for sub-10ms lookup. Real-time features (clicks in the last 5 minutes, current query) need stream processing (Flink over Kafka) and sub-ms Redis. The nightmare: a batch feature used in a real-time context serving stale values, or a real-time computation blowing the P99 budget. Enforcing this boundary architecturally is one of the most important design decisions.

A/B testing here has interference effects: recommending an item to treatment users changes its popularity, affecting control users. This breaks the independence A/B testing assumes. Solutions — user-level, item-level, or time-based splitting — each carry different biases you must understand to interpret results.

Part 3: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

RAG is heavily discussed and commonly implemented wrong, because most descriptions stop at "retrieve documents, add to prompt" and skip the engineering that determines whether it works.

Why it exists: LLMs have a training cutoff and no access to your proprietary data. Retraining is expensive; fine-tuning helps style, not facts. RAG gives the model a lookup capability instead — retrieve relevant documents at query time, inject them into context, and let the model reason over fresh, grounded evidence rather than memorized facts.

Chunking isn't a minor detail. Too small (under 100 tokens) and chunks lose context; too large (over 1,000) and you dilute signal. Best practice for most text: 256–512 tokens with 50-token overlap so concepts spanning a boundary survive.

Embedding quality determines retrieval quality — and high MTEB scores don't guarantee performance on your domain. Evaluate candidates on your actual queries (recall@10, MRR) before committing. Leading models mid-2026: OpenAI text-embedding-3-large, Cohere Embed v3, Voyage voyage-3-large, open-source bge-m3 and e5-mistral-7b.

Hybrid retrieval (the critical, usually-skipped step). Pure vector search is good at semantics but poor at exact match — error codes, SKUs, version strings, unusual names break it. Run BM25 (keyword) and vector search in parallel, then fuse with Reciprocal Rank Fusion: score = 1 / (rank + 60), summed across lists. Documents ranking high in both get a boost; the constant 60 is empirically robust.

Reranking. Pass the top 20–50 to a cross-encoder, which processes query and document jointly (unlike independent embeddings), capturing relevance nuance. Slower (10–100ms for 20) so applied only to a shortlist. Top rerankers 2026: Cohere Rerank 3.5, Voyage rerank-2.5, open-source bge-reranker-v2-m3. Sweet spot: 15–30 candidates.

Generation. Instruct the model to answer only from context and say "I don't know" otherwise — without this, LLMs hallucinate plausibly. Ask for source citations to make outputs auditable. Mind "lost in the middle": models attend to the start and end of long context, so place the most relevant chunks there, not buried.

Evaluation — the part most teams skip. A RAG system is only as good as its retrieval. Measure before obsessing over which LLM: Recall@K (if recall@5 is 0.60, 40% of queries get no answer in context and the LLM hallucinates), MRR (is the best chunk ranked early?), and faithfulness (does the answer stay within retrieved context? — RAGAS and DeepEval measure it). Build 50–100 question-answer pairs with known sources from the start and rerun after every change to chunking, embedding, K, or reranker.

Connecting the Patterns

All three work in demos and break in production. Lambda breaks because two codebases for one logic always diverge. Recommendations break because embedding quality is undertested and batch/real-time feature boundaries blur. RAG breaks because pure vector search misses exact matches and nobody measured retrieval recall. The pattern matters less than the discipline: measure what matters, instrument every stage, and never assume the system works just because it returns answers.
